# Combat Resolution

How combat is structured and resolved in the tSAC system.

Combat in tSAC is intended to feel **dangerous, fast-moving, and consequential**. Violence should carry weight both mechanically and narratively.

The system emphasizes **decision-making, positioning, and situational awareness** over long chains of modifiers or mechanical complexity.

---

# Design Intent

Combat in tSAC is designed to achieve several goals:

• **Dangerous and consequential encounters** — even experienced characters must respect violence.

• **Tactical choice without excessive bookkeeping** — players should think about positioning, timing, and intent rather than managing large stacks of modifiers.

• **Narrative outcomes** — combat does not exist purely as a mathematical exchange of damage. The outcome of violence should shape the story.

• **Psychological impact** — the system acknowledges that violence affects the mind as well as the body.

These principles guide the structure described below.

See also: [System Philosophy](philosophy.md)

# Combat Structure

Combat is divided into **rounds**, representing several seconds of intense activity.

Within each round, every participant has the opportunity to act in initiative order.

---

# Initiative

At the start of combat, each participant determines initiative.

Roll:

**2d6 + Agility + Wit**

Initiative represents both:

• **physical reaction speed** (Agility)  
• **situational awareness and decision-making speed** (Wit)

Participants act from highest result to lowest.

Initiative may be re-rolled in certain circumstances at the Game Guide's discretion (for example, when a new group enters combat or the environment dramatically changes).

---

# Action Economy

Each round, a character may perform:

**One Primary Action**

Primary actions represent major efforts requiring focus or commitment.

Examples include:

- Attacking an opponent
- Performing a complex maneuver
- Using a skill in combat
- Interacting with significant environmental elements

---

**One Secondary Action**

Secondary actions represent quick or low-effort activities.

Examples include:

- Moving a short distance
- Drawing or stowing a weapon
- Communicating a brief command
- Adjusting positioning

---

**One Reaction**

Reactions occur in response to another character's action.

Examples include:

- Defensive maneuver
- Dodge
- Opportunistic strike
- Counteraction

Once a reaction has been used, it cannot be used again until the character's next turn.

This limitation ensures that combat remains dynamic and prevents infinite defensive chains.

---

# Attacks and Defense

Combat attacks represent attempts to overcome an opponent's defenses through force, precision, or positioning.

---

## Making an Attack

Roll:

**Relevant Attribute + 2d6**

The attribute used depends on the attack type:

| Attack Type | Typical Attribute |
|--------------|------------------|
| Melee | Strength |
| Ranged | Agility |
| Specialized or unusual attacks | Determined by Game Guide |

The attack result is compared against the target's **Defense Rating**.

Defense Rating is typically calculated as:

**Agility + armor + situational modifiers**

---

## Degree of Success

Combat outcomes are determined by how much the attack roll exceeds (or fails to exceed) the target's Defense Rating.

| Result | Outcome |
|--------|---------|
| Below Defense | Miss — no effect |
| Equal to Defense | Glancing hit — reduced effect |
| Above Defense | Hit — full effect |
| 5+ above Defense | Clean hit — enhanced effect |

This structure allows combat to remain simple while still rewarding precision and strong execution.

---

# Damage

Successful attacks reduce a target's **Stamina**.

Damage is influenced by:

- The weapon's base damage
- The degree of success
- Relevant attribute bonuses

The exact damage formula may vary depending on weapon type and system refinement.

The important principle is that **better execution produces better outcomes**, rather than relying entirely on weapon statistics.

---

# Stamina and Combat

Stamina represents a character's ability to remain active during physical conflict.

It reflects:

- physical endurance
- pain tolerance
- breath control
- fatigue accumulation

As Stamina decreases, characters become less capable of performing complex actions.

| Stamina Level | Effect |
|---------------|--------|
| 75–100% | No penalty |
| 50–74% | Minor penalty to complex actions |
| 25–49% | Moderate penalty; reactions require effort |
| 1–24% | Severe penalty; actions require Willpower to maintain |
| 0 | Incapacitated |

Incapacitated characters cannot continue fighting without assistance.

Importantly, **incapacitation is not automatically death**.

---

# Willpower in Combat

Willpower normally remains stable during mundane combat.

However, certain circumstances may involve Willpower checks.

Examples include:

• witnessing horrific violence  
• resisting supernatural terror  
• confronting metaphysical entities  
• interacting with Protector-level phenomena  

Supernatural combat may involve both **Stamina and Willpower pressures simultaneously**.

---

# Ending Combat

Combat typically ends when the tactical situation is resolved.

Common endings include:

- One side is incapacitated
- One side surrenders
- One side flees
- The objective of the conflict has been achieved or lost
- An external event interrupts the encounter

The system assumes that combat is **contextual**, not an isolated mechanical mini-game.

---

# Death and Injury

Reaching zero Stamina does **not automatically kill a character**.

Death occurs only under specific circumstances such as:

- deliberate killing blows
- catastrophic injuries without treatment
- severe narrative conditions
